I Tried Brand Design… Here's What Happened

Jonathan Mupini December 2025 6 min read

Understanding the business first

Before touching a single design tool, the first question is always: what does this business actually do, and what does it stand for?

Ishealienaya Investments operates in two closely related spaces. The first is freight logistics, moving goods across Zimbabwe's fast-paced commercial environment, with a fleet of trucks available for hire across a wide variety of cargo requirements. The second is a driver training school that certifies professional drivers to the highest standards of road safety and operational expertise.

These two services are complementary. The same commitment to reliability, professionalism, and precision that makes their trucks dependable is what makes their training school credible.

That duality had to live inside the logo.

The core design challenge

Logistics brands face a specific visual challenge. They need to communicate two things simultaneously: scale and trustworthiness. Scale because they move things, often heavy things, across significant distances. Trustworthiness because clients are handing over cargo that matters to them.

The easiest path is a truck icon. But a standalone truck icon is generic, it tells you what the company does without telling you anything about who they are.

The better path is to make the identity distinctive enough to be owned.

The concept: monogram meets machinery

The logo concept I developed integrates three letters, I, A, and A (the initials of Ishealienaya Investments), with the side profile of two trucks.

The letters are not simply placed next to a truck. They are structurally merged with it. The shapes of the initials form the visual architecture of the trucks themselves, so the logo reads as both a monogram and an industry mark depending on how closely you look.

The best logos work at two levels: immediate recognition at a glance, and discovered meaning on closer inspection. The IAA monogram-truck integration was designed to reward that second look.

This approach achieves something important for a company with an unusual and distinctive name: it gives the brand a visual shorthand that is both memorable and meaningful, without requiring the full name to be legible at small sizes or on the side of a truck at speed.

Colour system

The palette was built around a primary action colour and a supporting range that communicates both industrial strength and approachability.

Brand Colour Palette

#EC4541, Action red. Energy, urgency, forward motion.
#3874BA, Steel blue. Reliability, professionalism, depth.
#5C5F6E, Charcoal. Neutral anchor for text and backgrounds.
#85AAD4, Mid blue. Supporting tone for secondary elements.
#A9C3E1, Light blue. Backgrounds, subtle highlights.
#FFFFFF, White. Clean space for text-heavy applications.

The dominant red communicates the energy and urgency a logistics business needs, this is a company that moves quickly and delivers on time. The steel blue family anchors it in trustworthiness and permanence. Together they create a palette that works on vehicle livery, uniforms, digital media, and printed materials without losing contrast or legibility.

Typography

Two typefaces define the brand's written voice.

Logo font: Bebas Neue Regular

Bebas Neue is a condensed, all-caps display typeface. It is bold without being aggressive, and its geometric construction echoes the angular shapes in the logo mark. At large sizes, on the side of a truck, on a billboard, in a vehicle livery, it reads instantly and powerfully. It also has strong digital legibility for headers and banners.

Primary font: Futura Book BT

Futura is one of the foundational geometric sans-serif typefaces. Book weight keeps it readable at body text sizes. It shares the geometric DNA of Bebas Neue without competing with it, ensuring consistency across formal documents, training materials, and marketing collateral. Together the two fonts create a typographic system that scales from a business card to a warehouse signage panel.

Logo variations

The identity was delivered with two primary treatments:

  • Primary logo: full-colour version for standard brand applications, digital media, documents, light backgrounds
  • Inverted logo: white-on-dark version for vehicle livery, dark uniform panels, branded merchandise, and night-visibility applications

Both versions maintain the same monogram-truck integration at different contrast ratios, ensuring the mark reads clearly on any surface the brand might appear on.

Mockup applications

The design was stress-tested across real-world surface mockups to confirm that the identity holds up beyond a white artboard:

  • Truck side-panel livery, verifying legibility at distance and at speed
  • Corporate stationery, confirming the mark works at small sizes on business cards and letterheads
  • Branded apparel, testing the inverted mark on dark fabric
  • Digital applications, verifying clarity on mobile screens and social media profile formats

Mockup testing is not just aesthetic review. It is a functional check. A logo that looks perfect on a screen can fail completely on embroidered fabric if the line weights are too fine or the letterforms too complex. Testing across surfaces is part of the work, not an afterthought.

What I learned from this project

The Ishealienaya identity reinforced something I come back to on every visual design brief: constraint is generative.

The constraint here was that the logo had to work on the side of a moving truck, large, far away, in sunlight, in rain, at night. That constraint eliminated dozens of directions that might have looked interesting on screen. It narrowed the field to what would actually survive contact with the real world.

The monogram-truck integration emerged from that constraint. It was not the first idea. It was the idea that remained after everything fragile had been eliminated.

Design for the hardest surface first. If your logo works on the side of a truck in the rain, it will work everywhere else. If it only works as a PNG on a white background, you have only solved half the problem.
